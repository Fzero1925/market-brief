Essential trading terminology explained

Basics

Bull Market

A market condition where prices are rising or expected to rise. Typically defined as a 20% increase from recent lows. The opposite of a bear market.

Basics

Bear Market

A market condition where prices are falling or expected to fall. Typically defined as a 20% decline from recent highs. The opposite of a bull market.

Trading

Pre-Market Trading

Trading that occurs before the regular market session opens (9:30 AM ET). Pre-market hours typically run from 4:00 AM to 9:30 AM ET. Provides early indication of market sentiment.

Trading

After-Hours Trading

Trading that occurs after the regular market closes (4:00 PM ET). After-hours typically runs from 4:00 PM to 8:00 PM ET. Used for reacting to earnings reports and news released after close.

Trading

Gap Up / Gap Down

When a stock opens significantly higher (gap up) or lower (gap down) than its previous closing price. Gaps often occur due to overnight news, earnings, or market events.

Orders

Market Order

An order to buy or sell a stock immediately at the best available current price. Guarantees execution but not price.

Orders

Limit Order

An order to buy or sell a stock at a specific price or better. Buy limit orders execute at or below the limit price; sell limit orders execute at or above the limit price.

Orders

Stop Loss

An order placed to automatically sell a stock when it reaches a certain price, designed to limit an investor's loss on a position.

Analysis

Volume

The number of shares traded during a given period. High volume often confirms the strength of a price move, while low volume suggests weak conviction.

Analysis

Market Cap

Market capitalization - the total value of a company's outstanding shares. Calculated by multiplying stock price by shares outstanding. Categories: Large-cap (> $10B), Mid-cap ($2B-$10B), Small-cap (< $2B).

Analysis

P/E Ratio

Price-to-Earnings ratio. A valuation metric calculated by dividing stock price by earnings per share. Higher P/E suggests investors expect higher future growth.

Earnings

EPS

Earnings Per Share. Calculated by dividing a company's profit by outstanding shares. A key metric for evaluating company profitability.

Earnings

Earnings Surprise

When a company's actual earnings differ from analysts' expectations. Positive surprises often drive stock prices higher; negative surprises can cause declines.

Fundamentals

Dividend

A portion of a company's earnings distributed to shareholders, usually quarterly. Dividend yield = Annual dividend / Stock price.

Fundamentals

Stock Split

When a company increases the number of its shares to boost liquidity. In a 2-for-1 split, shareholders receive 2 shares for every 1 held, with the price halved.

Indices

S&P 500

Standard & Poor's 500 - an index of 500 large-cap US stocks. Widely regarded as the best gauge of large-cap US equities.

Indices

Nasdaq Composite

An index of more than 3,000 stocks listed on the Nasdaq exchange, heavily weighted toward technology companies.

Indices

Dow Jones Industrial Average

A price-weighted index of 30 large, well-established US companies ("blue-chip" stocks). One of the oldest and most widely followed indices.

Risk

Volatility

A statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a security or market index. Higher volatility means larger price swings. The VIX index measures S&P 500 volatility.

Risk

Beta

A measure of a stock's volatility relative to the overall market. Beta of 1 means the stock moves with the market. Above 1 = more volatile; below 1 = less volatile.

Short Selling

Short Selling

Borrowing shares to sell them, with the expectation of buying them back later at a lower price to return to the lender. Profits from price declines.

Short Selling

Short Squeeze

A rapid increase in a stock's price due to short sellers buying shares to cover their positions. Can cause explosive upward price movements.
